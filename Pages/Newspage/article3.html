Bato dela Rosa asks Supreme Court
to block his ICC warrant

Jairo Bolledo


Nov 13, 2025 6:18 PM PHT




MANILA, Philippines – The International Criminal Court (ICC) warrant ordering the arrest of Senator Ronald “Bato” dela Rosa
has yet to be made public, but the senator already wants the Supreme Court (SC) to prevent it from being implemented.


Dela Rosa — with ICC-detained former president Rodrigo Duterte as a co-petitioner — filed a petition for
certiorari and prohibition with the SC to request the High Court to issue a temporary restraining order (TRO)
against his reported impending arrest.



In the petition filed on Thursday, November 13, the former Philippine National Police (PNP) chief – who was the
first to implement Duterte’s drug war that killed nearly 30,000, according to human rights groups — wants the Marcos government to:

  • Avoid enforcing the ICC warrant, red notice, or surrender request against Dela Rosa
  • Avoid extending any form of assistance to ICC witnesses
  • Refrain from transmitting any ICC-related communication correspondence pending final resolution of the case


The PNP chief-turned-lawmaker played a vital role in the drug war as he is considered its “architect.” Between
July 2016 to October 2018, covering most of Dela Rosa’s time as PNP chief, at least 4,999 had died, according to
Philippine Drug Enforcement Agency’s data.



"On November 8, former Department of Justice (DOJ) and now Ombudsman Jesus Crispin “Boying” Remulla
said the ICC has already issued a warrant of arrest against Dela Rosa over his involvement in the war on drugs.





In a separate petition with the SC, Dela Rosa’s camp also wants to compel Remulla to submit to the Court the supposed warrant he’s referring to.


“Wherefore, it is respectfully prayed that this Honorable Court order Respondent Ombudsman Jesus Crispin Remulla
to immediately submit a copy of the alleged ICC warrant he claims to possess on his mobile phone and direct him to
file a sworn explanation stating how he obtained the alleged document, from whom, when, and under what authority,” Dela Rosa’s petition read.


Remulla, during a press briefing with reporters on November 10, reiterated that there’s already a warrant against Dela Rosa.


“I have the warrant on my phone. But it’s not an official copy. [They] will go to the proper channels to implement it,” the Ombudsman said.


ICC-accredited lawyer Kristina Conti explained that there had been an amendment to ICC rules, requiring that all warrant
requests should be filed by the prosecutor as “secret.”


“What this means is that, whether a warrant has indeed been issued or is still to be issued, it is most likely not
intended to be made known to the public — until of course a suspect has been brought to the court,” Conti said. “Considerations
for publicizing or not publicizing a warrant include whether this could interrupt criminal conduct, deter the commission of
other crimes, or maximize opportunities for arrest.”


Extradition is the “removal of an accused from the Philippines with the object of placing him at the disposal of
foreign authorities to enable the requesting state or government to hold him in connection with any criminal investigation
directed against him or the execution of a penalty imposed on him under the penal or criminal law of the
requesting state or government.”


“Under Rule 2, Section 4 (of the newly approved rules), the Court must first make a judicial determination of
probable cause before any warrant of arrest may issue for extradition or surrender. Rule 2, Section 158 further
provides that no surrender or turnover shall take place unless there is final judgment by the Court,” Dela Rosa argued.


DOJ officials, on November 11, said the Philippines can surrender Dela Rosa to The Hague, Netherlands,
if not through extradition. The officials said surrender is the faster route and under Republic Act No. 9851 or
the Philippine Act on Crimes Against International Humanitarian Law, Genocide, and Other Crimes Against Humanity,
the country can extradite or surrender a person wanted by an international court.


For some lawyers like international law expert Ross Tugade, the newly approved extradition rules do not apply to Dela Rosa’s case.


“The SC rules, and extradition as a process in general, strictly refers to another state requesting the presence of a person in their
jurisdiction to answer for crimes. The ICC is not a state; it is a treaty-established court,” Tugade told Rappler.
